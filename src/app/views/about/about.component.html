Introduction to the PED

The structural characterization of intrinsically disordered proteins (IDPs) has obvious inherent difficulties compared to proteins with more rigid structures. Structural information of a single conformation is often insufficient to get an insight on the functions carried out by IDPs; therefore these proteins can better be described as ensembles of a high number of conformations. These ensembles are calculated based on experimental data coming mainly from Nuclear Magnetic Resonance (NMR) and Small-angle X-ray Scattering (SAXS) measurements, and provide valuable functional information that would otherwise prove difficult to elucidate using classical approaches. However, meaningful ensembles might also be constructed using Molecular Dynamics (MD) simulations, therefore PED is willing to host such ensembles too, however, experimental validation is advised for these models. The goal of the PED project is to provide a platform for the IDP community where ensembles and their corresponding primary data can be stored and used as benchmarking datasets to facilitate the development of new ensemble calculation methods.

It is possible to deposit new ensembles to PED. To do so, please read the information for data owners available from the deposition process section below.

What is an ensemble?

Introduction to structural ensembles

The shear number of conformers that coexist in solution for a flexible system precludes their description with X-ray crystallography. However, Nuclear Magnetic Resonance (NMR) and Small Angle X-ray Scattering (SAXS) experiments can provide geometric constraints that can be used in the selection of an ensemble of conformations that approximately describe the conformational dynamics of a flexible system in solution. There are two broad approaches to generating disordered state ensembles that fit experimental data. The first is called ensemble-restrained molecular dynamics (MD) where MD simulations are driven by a set of restraints so that a set of representative conformers for the data. The second is more recent and involves the pre-generation of random coil-like conformations and selecting subsets that fit the data. Although the latter approach is described in more detail here, PED is also hosting ensembles from MD simulations, both experimentally constrained, and unconstrained. However, experimental validation is always advised.

Generating the starting pool

The most common procedure of ensemble calculation starts with generating a vast starting pool of conformations. These conformations can be completely random coil-like or may already be constrained by experimental or theoretical data such as psi/phi angles, local or nonlocal contacts or secondary structure propensities. Some programs commonly used for this step are Flexible-Meccano (FM), RanCh and TRaDES. MD simulations may also be used to provide a starting pool. The conformers generated may need to be completed, for example FM conformers lack side-chains that need to be modeled in afterwards with software such as SCCOMP or SCRWL.

Back-calculated data

After generating the starting pool, a variety of data-types are back-calculated from the conformers in order to enable comparison with experimental data. For SAXS data, the software CRYSOL calculates scattering curves, averaging individual intensities for each conformer. For NMR data, FM can estimate Residual Dipolar Coupling values (RDCs), and Paramagnetic Relaxation Enhancement (PRE) and J-coupling values for the generated conformer pools. On the other hand, ENSEMBLE utilizes CRYSOL for SAXS data, HYDROPRO for NMR-derived Rh data, ShiftX for chemical shift data, a local-alignment approach for RDCs, and internal scripts for solvent accessibility, PREs, J-couplings, R2 relaxation rates and NOEs.

Fitting to the experimental data

The aim of the ensemble calculation is to select a subset of conformers whose back-calculated values fit the actual experimental data coming from the SAXS and NMR measurements. The software Gajoe, part of EOM, deals with the selection of the pool of conformers that fit the theoretical and experimental SAXS curves best. The program ASTEROIDS carries out a similar selection based on NMR parameters. ENSEMBLE similarly can select a subset of conformers on the basis of SAXS and a variety of different NMR data. The size of the final ensembles may range from only a few to hundreds of conformers; after a threshold, increasing the ensemble size may not improve the fit any further and the "final" ensembles are conformationally redundant.

Limitations and perspectives

As the final structural ensembles are models, there are several caveats to accepting them as true representations of structural reality. First, the quality of the final ensemble depends strongly on the quality of the experimental data. Aggregation, degradation or purity issues can severely affect the reliability of the experimental data used to determine the ensemble structure of an IDP. In case of techniques such as SAXS, experiments always yield results, even in case of severe errors. Therefore data has to be carefully examined and controlled. Furthermore, even in an ideal case it is currently impossible to describe the system with a single structural ensemble. Determining an ensemble is an ill-posed problem, and pools of different conformers may fit the experimental data equally well (ie. there is no unique solution). Lastly, to be able to carry out large-scale analysis on the conformational ensembles, one has to make sure that the modeling of the conformers is of acceptable quality, therefore, similarly to globular structures, stereochemical validation has become part of the validation steps. One purpose of the PED is to scrutinize these calculated ensembles for various measures of information content and quality. Even with all their shortcomings, structural ensembles represent our current best approach for describing the structure of IDPs.

Deposition process

PED will add new entries as a part of periodic releases. In the period between releases, new entries can be deposited at any time. Candidate entries will be manually reviewed by our biocurators for quality assurance and, following approval, they will become public in the next release of the database. Structural ensembles can be deposited in PEDdeposition at any stage of publication. Data owners providing us with ensembles from manuscripts in preparation will be provided with a temporary key‐string encoded link, for private dissemination with journal reviewers and editors. Data deposited from manuscripts in preparation or from any stage of pre-publication will become public starting from the next release of the database, following review by biocurators and approval by the data owner.

Controlled Vocabulary

In order to enhance the browsability of the database and to standardize the keywords describing the experimental methodology to determine the ensembles, a database specific controlled vocabulary (CV) is under development. The three aspects of this CV are:

  1. Measurement method
  2. Ensemble generation method
  3. Molecular dynamics

The aspect Measurement method is further divided into subclasses referring to the most common techniques. These techniques have many different spectra and parameters to be measured and various different ways to evaluate the readouts. The aspect Ensemble generation method mostly lists software tools used to model and assess the conformations of the ensemble. These are classified into subcategories corresponding to different steps of the ensemble generation procedures: Initial pool, Structure calculation, Ensemble optimization, and additionally, Auxiliary tools. The third aspect, Molecular dynamics, lists simulations programs and force fields. It is to be noted that terms belonging to this category might be used to detail long simulations as well as short energy minimizations that represent two substantially different purposes.
